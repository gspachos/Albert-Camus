Albert Camus

The philosopher and writer who delved into existential questions of meaning and absurdity in human existence

Basic timeline of his life:

  • 1913 - Born in Mondovi, French Algeria
  • 1933 - Attends the University of Algiers, where he becomes involved in theater and journalism
  • 1939 - Publishes his first collection of essays, "L'Envers et l'endroit" ("The Wrong Side and the Right Side")
  • 1942 - Publishes his novel "L'Étranger" ("The Stranger"), which explores themes of existentialism and absurdity
  • 1947 - Writes the play "Caligula"
  • 1948 - Publishes the novel "La Peste" ("The Plague"), a allegorical exploration of the human condition in the face of suffering and absurdity
  • 1951 - Receives the Nobel Prize in Literature for his literary contributions, especially "La Peste" and his philosophical essays
  • 1956 - Publishes his essay "L'Homme révolté" ("The Rebel"), in which he examines the nature of rebellion and revolution
  • 1960 - Dies in a car accident at the age of 46, leaving behind a legacy of existentialist thought and literary works that continue to influence philosophy and literature
